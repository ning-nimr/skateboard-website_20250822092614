The History of Skateboarding

From sidewalk surfing to Olympic sport: Explore the evolution of skateboarding culture

The Evolution of Skateboarding

From humble beginnings to global phenomenon

1940s-1950s: The Birth

Skateboarding originated in California when surfers sought an activity for days without waves. Early skateboards were homemade, consisting of wooden boxes or planks with roller skate wheels attached. This practice became known as "sidewalk surfing."

1960s: First Commercial Boards

The first commercial skateboards appeared in 1959, but it was in the early 1960s that manufacturers like Makaha and Hobie began producing boards resembling small surfboards. In 1963, Makaha formed the first professional skateboard team and organized the first skateboard competition in Hermosa Beach, California.

1970s: The Polyurethane Revolution

A significant revival occurred in the early 1970s with the invention of the polyurethane wheel by Frank Nasworthy in 1972. These "Cadillac Wheels" offered better traction and smoother rides. Additionally, Larry Stevenson patented the kicktail in 1969, enhancing control and enabling new tricks.

Mid-1970s: The Z-Boys

The mid-1970s saw the emergence of the Zephyr Skateboard Team, or Z-Boys, in Venice, California. Members like Tony Alva introduced aggressive, surf-inspired styles to skateboarding, pioneering vertical skating by riding in empty swimming pools. Alva is credited with being one of the first skateboarders to successfully perform a frontside air.

1978: The Ollie

In 1978, Alan "Ollie" Gelfand invented the "ollie," a trick where the rider jumps into the air with the board without using their hands. This maneuver became foundational for modern street skating and led to the development of numerous other tricks.

1980s-1990s: Street Skating & Culture

The rise of street skating, fueled by skaters like Rodney Mullen, who adapted the ollie to flat ground and invented tricks like the kickflip, brought the sport to urban environments. Skateboarding videos and magazines proliferated, and brands like Supreme emerged from the New York City skate scene.

2000s: Mainstream Acceptance

In the 2000s, skateboarding's popularity continued to grow, with more skateparks being built and the sport gaining mainstream acceptance. Video games like Tony Hawk's Pro Skater introduced skateboarding to millions of non-skaters worldwide.

2020: Olympic Debut

The inclusion of skateboarding in the 2020 Tokyo Olympics marked a significant milestone, showcasing the sport's global appeal and athleticism. Skateboarding's Olympic debut has further elevated its status, with events planned for the Paris 2024 and Los Angeles 2028 Games.
